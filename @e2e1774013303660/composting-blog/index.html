What is Composting?

Composting is nature's way of recycling organic materials into nutrient-rich soil amendment. Through the work of microorganisms, insects, and worms, kitchen scraps and yard waste transform into dark, crumbly compost that gardeners call "black gold." This natural process reduces waste sent to landfills while creating a valuable resource for your garden.

Benefits of Composting

🌿 Environmentally Friendly

Reduces methane emissions from landfills and decreases your carbon footprint by diverting organic waste.

💰 Cost-Effective

Eliminates the need to purchase expensive commercial fertilizers and soil amendments.

🌱 Improves Soil Health

Enriches soil with essential nutrients, improves drainage, and enhances water retention.

🐛 Supports Biodiversity

Creates habitat for beneficial organisms and promotes healthy soil ecosystems.

What You Can and Can't Compost

✅ Compost These:

  • Fruit and vegetable scraps
  • Coffee grounds and filters
  • Tea bags (remove staples)
  • Eggshells (crushed)
  • Grass clippings
  • Leaves and small twigs
  • Shredded newspaper and cardboard
  • Houseplant trimmings
  • Sawdust (untreated wood)
  • Hair and pet fur

❌ Avoid These:

  • Meat, fish, or poultry
  • Dairy products
  • Oils and fats
  • Pet waste
  • Diseased plants
  • Invasive weeds with seeds
  • Treated wood or sawdust
  • Coal or charcoal ash
  • Non-organic materials
  • Bread products (attract pests)

Getting Started: Step-by-Step Guide

Choose Your Composting Method

Select an approach that fits your space and lifestyle. Outdoor compost bins work great for yards, while indoor vermicomposting (worm bins) is perfect for apartments. Tumbler systems offer faster decomposition, while simple pile methods require less investment.

Pick the Perfect Location

Place your compost bin in a partially shaded area with good drainage. The spot should be convenient to access year-round but not too close to living spaces. Ensure it's on bare soil to allow beneficial organisms to enter and excess moisture to drain.

Build Your Compost Pile

Start with a layer of coarse materials like twigs for drainage. Alternate between "green" nitrogen-rich materials (food scraps, grass clippings) and "brown" carbon-rich materials (dry leaves, paper). Aim for a 3:1 ratio of browns to greens.

Maintain Your Compost

Turn your compost every 1-2 weeks to aerate and speed decomposition. Keep the pile moist like a wrung-out sponge. Monitor temperature - active compost should feel warm. Add water if too dry, more browns if too wet or smelly.

Harvest Your Compost

Compost is ready when it's dark, crumbly, and earthy-smelling (typically 2-6 months). Screen out large pieces that haven't broken down yet. Use your finished compost in gardens, potted plants, or as a natural fertilizer for your lawn.

Troubleshooting Common Issues

Foul Odors

Usually caused by too many greens or poor aeration. Add more brown materials and turn the pile more frequently. Ensure proper drainage and avoid adding meat or dairy products.

Slow Decomposition

Pile might be too dry, too small, or lack nitrogen. Add water to maintain moisture, increase pile size, or add more green materials. Chop materials into smaller pieces for faster breakdown.

Pest Problems

Bury food scraps under brown materials and avoid adding meat, oils, or bread. Use a bin with a tight-fitting lid or wire mesh bottom to deter rodents while allowing drainage.

Using Your Finished Compost

Your nutrient-rich compost is incredibly versatile. Mix it into garden soil before planting, use it as mulch around trees and shrubs, or create compost tea for watering plants. Spread a thin layer on lawns as natural fertilizer, or blend with potting soil for container gardens. The possibilities are endless for improving soil health and plant growth naturally.
